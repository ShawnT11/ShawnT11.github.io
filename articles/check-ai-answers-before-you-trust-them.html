How to Check an AI Answer Before You Trust It

AI answers can be useful without being reliable. The work is learning how to separate a helpful lead from a claim you can actually stand behind.

The AI Explainer editorial Updated July 2026 8 min read
Use when An AI answer will influence a decision, document, recommendation, or public claim.
Core habit Check the claim, not the confidence of the wording.
Stop point If you cannot verify a claim, rewrite it as uncertainty or remove it.

Start by identifying the claims

The first pass is not fact-checking. It is claim-finding. Read the answer and mark every sentence that says something specific about the world: a number, date, quote, rule, product behavior, legal requirement, scientific result, source, or recommendation.

General advice can still be weak, but specific claims create the highest risk. They look useful because they are concrete. That is exactly why they deserve more attention.

Sort claims by consequence

Not every sentence needs the same level of review. A brainstormed headline can be imperfect. A compliance note, a medical explanation, a hiring recommendation, a financial assumption, or a technical migration plan cannot be treated casually.

  • Low consequence: tone options, rough outlines, internal brainstorming, naming ideas.
  • Medium consequence: customer emails, product copy, meeting notes, project plans.
  • High consequence: legal, medical, financial, security, safety, employment, or public factual claims.
The more a sentence can change someone else's decision, the less you should rely on the model's confidence.

Ask what would prove it wrong

A useful check is to reverse the answer. If the model says a tool has a feature, what page would prove it does not? If it says a policy requires something, where would the current policy say otherwise? If it summarizes a source, what exact paragraph should contain the evidence?

This habit changes the task from admiring a polished answer to testing a claim. It also helps you avoid vague verification, where you search until you find something that merely sounds similar.

Use the two-source rule carefully

Two weak sources do not make a strong source. A blog post repeating another blog post is not independent confirmation. For important claims, prefer primary documents, official documentation, original research, direct product pages, or sources with clear editorial accountability.

If the article will be published, quote less and link more. Your job is to make the path to verification visible without copying large sections of someone else's work.

Keep uncertainty in the final text

Sometimes the best edit is not finding a stronger source. It is changing the sentence. "This always works" may become "this can work when the tool supports export controls." "The law requires" may become "some policies require." "The safest approach" may become "a lower-risk approach."

Good AI editing is not only about catching errors. It is about restoring the amount of certainty the evidence actually supports.
