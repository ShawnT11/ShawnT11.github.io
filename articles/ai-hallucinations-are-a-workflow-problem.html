Hallucinations

AI Hallucinations Are a Workflow Problem

A hallucination is not only a strange model behavior. It is a failure mode in the surrounding workflow: a confident answer reaches a person, document, or decision before the right checks happen.

The AI Explainer editorial Updated July 2026 8 min read
Problem Fluent answers can contain invented facts, links, quotes, or details.
Response Design review steps around claim checking, not around how polished the answer sounds.
Rule The model can suggest. The workflow must verify.

Why hallucinations are hard to catch

People often expect errors to look messy. AI errors are different. They can arrive inside a polished paragraph with clean grammar, plausible structure, and a tone that sounds certain. That makes the mistake harder to notice than a typo or broken sentence.

The danger is not that every output is wrong. The danger is that the output does not always tell you which parts are grounded and which parts are guesses. A sentence can be useful, approximate, outdated, or invented without changing its voice.

Do not make the model grade itself

Asking a model whether its own answer is accurate may catch obvious problems, but it is not enough. The same system that produced the weak claim may produce a confident defense of it. For important work, verification has to point outside the model.

  • Check quoted language against the original source.
  • Open links instead of trusting their titles.
  • Confirm product behavior in current documentation.
  • Test code, formulas, and instructions in a controlled environment.
The safest AI workflow assumes the answer may be useful and still not ready.

Separate three kinds of output

Some output is creative material: names, angles, examples, headlines, and first drafts. Some output is structured help: tables, outlines, checklists, and summaries. Some output is factual claim: dates, rules, statistics, quotes, and technical specifics.

These categories need different review. Creative material needs taste. Structured help needs completeness. Factual claims need evidence.

Make verification part of the prompt

Ask for uncertainty

Tell the model to mark assumptions, unknowns, and claims that require source checking. This will not make the answer automatically true, but it improves the shape of the review.

Ask for a checking list

After a draft, ask which facts need verification before publication. Then do the checking yourself.

Ask for weaker wording

If a claim is uncertain, rewrite it with the right level of caution instead of pretending the evidence is stronger than it is.

Use AI where errors are visible

The easiest place to use AI safely is where you can see the quality of the result. If you know the subject, you can spot missing context. If the task is a draft, you can edit. If the output is a table built from your own notes, you can compare it to the source material.

The hardest place is where you ask the model to replace knowledge you do not have. That is when a hallucination can become invisible until it causes damage.
